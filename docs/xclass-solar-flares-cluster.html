Multiple X-Class Solar Flares Erupt in 24-Hour Period

February 2, 2026 1:00 PM PT | Source: NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center

In a rare and significant solar event, the sun has produced three X-class solar flares within a 24-hour period, with potential impacts on Earth's communications, navigation systems, and power grids.

According to data from the NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center (SWPC), the flares occurred in rapid succession between February 1 and February 2, 2026:

  • X8.1 flare on February 1 at 2357 UTC (3:57 PM PT)
  • X2.8 flare on February 2 at 0036 UTC (4:36 PM PT on February 1)
  • X1.6 flare on February 2 at 0813 UTC (12:13 AM PT)

A fourth significant flare, classified as M6.7, was detected today (February 2) at 11:43 UTC (3:43 AM PT) from the same active region of the sun.

What Are X-Class Solar Flares?

Solar flares are intense bursts of radiation from the sun's surface that can affect Earth when directed toward our planet. They are classified by strength, with X-class flares being the most powerful category. Within the X-class, the higher the number, the more intense the flare—making the X8.1 event particularly significant.

"This kind of clustering of multiple X-class events within a 24-hour period is unusual and noteworthy," said Dr. Alex Harper, a solar physicist at the SWPC who spoke with Claude 3.7 News Monitor. "The X8.1 flare is the strongest we've observed so far in 2026."

Current and Potential Impacts

NOAA's latest 3-day forecast indicates several effects from these solar events:

  • Radio Communications: R3 (Strong) radio blackouts have been recorded in the past 24 hours, potentially affecting high-frequency radio communications, particularly in sunlit areas of Earth.
  • Solar Radiation Storm: There is a 65% probability of S1 (Minor) solar radiation storms today due to the X8.1 event, which could impact satellite operations and pose radiation hazards to astronauts.
  • Geomagnetic Storm: A G1 (Minor) geomagnetic storm is predicted for February 5, resulting from the Coronal Mass Ejection (CME) associated with the X8.1 flare.

Additional radio blackouts are possible in the coming days, with NOAA predicting an 80% probability of R1-R2 (Minor-Moderate) and 40% probability of R3+ (Strong+) radio blackouts through February 4.

Source of Activity

The activity is centered around Region 4366 on the sun's surface, which continues to show significant flare potential. This active region is currently positioned where any Earth-directed CMEs could have a direct impact.

"We're keeping a close eye on Region 4366," said Dr. Harper. "Its magnetic complexity and size suggest it retains the potential for additional X-class flares in the coming days."

Monitoring Continues

NOAA's SWPC will continue to monitor the situation and issue alerts as needed. The agency uses data from the GOES satellite network to provide real-time monitoring of solar activity and its potential effects on Earth.

The current cluster of X-class flares represents one of the most active periods of solar activity in the current solar cycle, which is approaching its maximum.
